\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This chapter presents and critically analyses the experimental results obtained from the
LungInsight multimodal framework for lung nodule malignancy prediction and disease-stage
classification. The system integrates a DenseNet-121-based CT encoder with a
multi-layer-perceptron (MLP) clinical branch; the two representations are fused and decoded
by a shared prediction head that simultaneously outputs a malignancy probability and a
four-class stage label. Explainability is provided through Gradient-weighted Class Activation
Mapping (Grad-CAM) for the imaging branch and SHAP for the clinical branch.

Results are reported on the held-out test set ($n = 161$ nodules; 80 malignant, 81 benign)
from the matched LIDC-IDRI/NLST paired cohort described in Chapter~4. Section~\ref{sec:results}
presents quantitative performance; Section~\ref{sec:analysis} analyses those results in depth;
Section~\ref{sec:comparison} contextualises the findings against the prior literature;
Section~\ref{sec:findings} synthesises key takeaways; and Section~\ref{sec:limitations}
acknowledges study limitations and directions for future work.

qualitatively consistent with established lung cancer risk stratification instruments

for the clinical branch.

\section{Analysis of Results}
\label{sec:analysis}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Effect of Multimodal Fusion}

The most informative contrast in Table~\ref{tab:results_ablation} is between the full
model and the single-modality ablations. The imaging-only variant (ROC-AUC $0.897$,
sensitivity $0.851$) confirms that CT morphology alone carries substantial discriminative
signal; this is expected given the LIDC-IDRI expert consensus labels, which are
predominantly based on radiological appearance. However, removing the clinical branch
incurs a statistically significant drop of $0.024$ AUC units (bootstrap 95\% CI of the
difference: $[0.009,\,0.039]$), indicating that demographic and functional covariates
provide information not recoverable from the image alone.

The clinical-only ablation (ROC-AUC $0.803$) establishes the ceiling for a tabular-only
model on this cohort. While non-trivial, this performance is substantially below the
imaging branch, underscoring that CT texture and morphology are the dominant signal
for nodule-level malignancy. The marginal but consistent improvement of the full model
over imaging-only confirms the complementary nature of the two modalities rather than
simple redundancy.

\subsection{Effect of Training Objective Components}

Removing the XAI-consistency loss reduces ROC-AUC to $0.908$ and, more notably,
produces Grad-CAM activations that are spatially less coherent with the nodule masks
(qualitative assessment; quantitative localisation metrics are deferred to future work).
This suggests the auxiliary attention supervision provides a useful inductive bias even
when segmentation labels are available for only 63\% of the training cases.

Ablating the auxiliary stage classification loss preserves malignancy ROC-AUC
($0.914$) but degrades stage macro-F1 substantially, from $0.761$ to $0.698$. This
confirms that multi-task supervision contributes primarily to staging quality rather
than binary classification, and that the staging head can be disabled at inference time
without penalising malignancy discrimination.

\subsection{Effect of Backbone Fine-Tuning and Architecture}

The frozen-backbone variant (ROC-AUC $0.881$) underperforms the fully fine-tuned
model by $0.040$ AUC units, a gap larger than the modality ablation gap. This is
consistent with the domain shift between the ImageNet pretraining distribution and
grayscale 64$\times$64 nodule patches: the upper layers of a frozen DenseNet-121
contain filters tuned for natural image statistics that do not transfer directly to
pulmonary CT texture. The three-epoch warm-up protocol (backbone frozen, head trained
freely) followed by full joint fine-tuning therefore accounts for a meaningful fraction
of the system's performance.

Substituting ResNet-50 for DenseNet-121 yields ROC-AUC $0.904$, marginally below the
DenseNet variant. Both models share similar parameter counts at the scales used here
($\sim$7M and $\sim$8M respectively after head truncation), suggesting that the
densely connected feature reuse in DenseNet-121 provides a moderate structural
advantage for small, high-density medical patches.

\subsection{Effect of Data Augmentation}

Removing CT augmentation degrades ROC-AUC to $0.893$, confirming that the training
set size ($n = 752$ nodules) is insufficient for the DenseNet-121 encoder to learn
rotation-and-flip-invariant representations from data alone. The augmentation
pipeline—random axial flips, $\pm 15^{\circ}$ rotations, and Gaussian intensity jitter
($\sigma = 0.02$)—represents a deliberate, conservative choice: more aggressive
elastic deformations were trialled but produced marginal gains at the cost of
substantially longer training time.

Adding an auxiliary localisation loss on the 63\% of training cases with bounding-box
annotations produces a modest improvement to ROC-AUC $0.924$, within the confidence
interval of the full model. The more consequential benefit is spatial: Grad-CAM
centroid displacement falls from 3.2~mm to 2.1~mm on the true-positive test cases
(unpaired $t$-test $p = 0.031$), suggesting improved spatial consistency without
significantly affecting classification.

\subsection{Calibration and Clinical Utility}

Beyond discrimination metrics, a calibration analysis (Platt scaling applied on the
validation set) yields a Brier score of $0.118$ for the full model, compared to
$0.141$ for imaging-only and $0.197$ for clinical-only. Decision curve analysis
confirms positive net benefit relative to the treat-all and treat-none reference
strategies across the clinically relevant probability threshold range of $0.10$–$0.70$.
These supplementary analyses, presented in full in Appendix~B, support the deployment
of the system's output as a calibrated risk score rather than solely as a
hard-threshold classifier.

The imaging-only models of Hammad~et~al.\ and Alqahtani~et~al.\ demonstrate that
DL architectures with Grad-CAM overlays can achieve high binary-classification
accuracy on curated CT datasets. LungInsight builds on this baseline by incorporating
matched clinical covariates, demonstrating a measurable AUC gain over imaging-only
inference, and extending the output space to four-class staging. The multimodal
system of Oncu~\&~Ciftci is architecturally most similar, but does not report
Grad-CAM or SHAP explanations and provides insufficient detail for benchmarking.
The foundation-model approach of Niu~et~al.\ demonstrates the scalability of
multimodal pretraining for screening tasks but does not address interpretability, which
is a central requirement for clinical decision-support deployments under emerging
regulatory frameworks such as the EU AI Act (2024).

Compared with the survey of Chaudhari~\&~Gudadhe, which identifies the absence of
unified XAI pipelines and multimodal integration as the two principal open problems in
the field, LungInsight directly addresses both: it fuses CT and tabular modalities at
the late-fusion layer and couples two complementary XAI instruments, each
tailored to its respective input type.

\section{Discussion of Key Findings}
\label{sec:findings}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The experimental results yield five principal findings.

\begin{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Multimodal fusion produces measurable and consistent gains.}
    Combining DenseNet-121 CT features with an MLP clinical branch improves malignancy
    ROC-AUC by $0.024$ over imaging alone ($p < 0.05$, bootstrap) and by $0.118$
    over clinical data alone. The two modalities are complementary: CT morphology
    captures local nodule texture, spiculation, and density, while clinical covariates
    encode systemic cancer risk factors that are invisible to image-only models.

    \item \textbf{Grad-CAM confirms medically coherent spatial attention.}
    For the majority of correctly classified malignant cases, peak Grad-CAM activation
    co-localises with the radiologist-annotated nodule region (mean centroid
    displacement 3.2~mm, dropping to 2.1~mm with auxiliary localisation supervision).
    Activation failures are concentrated in sub-solid and part-solid nodules, which
    present reduced image contrast and are known to challenge radiologists as well
    as automated systems.

    \item \textbf{SHAP identifies clinically plausible risk drivers.}
    The top clinical predictors by mean $|\text{SHAP}|$---pack-years, emphysema grade,
    and FEV\textsubscript{1}/FVC---are established risk factors in epidemiological lung
    cancer models. This congruence provides evidence that the clinical branch has
    learned genuine statistical associations rather than dataset-specific artefacts.

    \item \textbf{Multi-task staging supervision improves stage prediction without
    penalising malignancy classification.}
    The auxiliary stage loss improves four-class macro-F1 from $0.698$ to $0.761$
    with negligible effect on malignancy ROC-AUC, demonstrating that the two tasks
    share a useful intermediate representation while remaining separable at the output
    layer.

    \item \textbf{A single end-to-end inference pipeline supports clinical deployment.}
    The Streamlit dashboard integrates DICOM ingestion, preprocessing, inference, and
    dual-modality explanation generation at $\approx 0.4$~s per case on commodity CPU
    hardware. This latency is compatible with existing radiological review workflows,
    where decisions are typically deferred until after report generation rather than
    being required in real time.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Limitations of the Study}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Several limitations must be acknowledged before the results can be interpreted in a
broader clinical or regulatory context.

\begin{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Retrospective, single-cohort evaluation.}
    All experiments are conducted on a retrospective matched cohort derived from two
    public datasets (LIDC-IDRI and NLST). Prospective, multi-centre validation on an
    independent cohort---including scanner heterogeneity, varying acquisition protocols,
    and populations not represented in US-based screening trials---is necessary before
    clinical deployment.

    \item \textbf{Cohort size and class balance.}
    The final paired cohort comprises 847 patients (1{,}074 nodules). While adequate
    for exploratory validation, this is insufficient to support subgroup analyses
    (e.g., by histological subtype, sex, or ethnicity) with adequate statistical power.
    The near-balanced malignant/benign ratio ($\approx$50:50) also does not reflect
    screening-population prevalence ($\approx$5--10\%), so reported sensitivity and
    specificity should be re-calibrated under realistic prevalence assumptions.

    \item \textbf{Staging label quality.}
    Disease-stage labels in the matched cohort are derived from NLST follow-up records
    rather than pathological staging reports. Stage~III and~IV cases are substantially
    underrepresented (11.1\% and 4.0\% respectively), which limits the reliability of
    macro-F1 as a staging metric and may introduce optimistic bias for the common stages.

    \item \textbf{Grad-CAM as a proxy for localisation quality.}
    Grad-CAM produces class-discriminative heatmaps but does not perform instance
    segmentation. Centroid displacement is used as a localisation surrogate, but this
    metric does not capture the spatial extent of activation relative to nodule volume.
    Pixel-level attribution methods (e.g., Integrated Gradients) or segmentation-coupled
    attention mechanisms would provide more rigorous localisation guarantees.

    \item \textbf{Clinical data completeness.}
    FEV\textsubscript{1}/FVC measurements are missing for 6.4\% of the cohort and
    are imputed by median value. In a prospective deployment, missing pulmonary function
    data would be more common, and the sensitivity of predictions to imputation strategy
    has not been systematically studied.

    \item \textbf{Regulatory and integration requirements.}
    Deployment in a clinical decision-support role requires prospective validation,
    bias audits across demographic subgroups, integration with picture archiving and
    communication systems (PACS), and regulatory clearance (e.g., FDA 510(k) or CE-MDR
    under EU MDR 2017/745). None of these requirements have been addressed in this work.

\end{enumerate}

Future work should prioritise prospective multi-centre validation, expansion of the
clinical covariate set to include genomic and proteomic biomarkers, exploration of
transformer-based CT encoders (e.g., SwinUNETR), and a systematic evaluation of
Integrated Gradients or attention-rollout methods as complements or replacements for
Grad-CAM in the imaging branch.
